\section{Objective}
Express orbital radius in anomaly variables and derive series expansions for $r^{-3}$ and $r^{-4}$.

\section{Radius Formulas}

From true anomaly:
\begin{equation}
r = \frac{a(1-e^2)}{1 + e\cos f} = \frac{p}{1 + e\cos f}
\end{equation}

From eccentric anomaly:
\begin{equation}
\boxed{r = a(1 - e\cos E)}
\label{eq:r_E}
\end{equation}

Kepler's equation:
\begin{equation}
M = E - e\sin E
\end{equation}

\section{Series Expansions}

From Eq.~\eqref{eq:r_E}, $r^{-1} = a^{-1}(1 - e\cos E)^{-1} \approx a^{-1}[1 + e\cos E + O(e^2)]$

Therefore:
\begin{align}
r^{-3} &\approx a^{-3}(1 + e\cos E)^3 \approx a^{-3}[1 + 3e\cos E + O(e^2)]\\
r^{-4} &\approx a^{-4}(1 + e\cos E)^4 \approx a^{-4}[1 + 4e\cos E + O(e^2)]
\end{align}

\section{Conversion to Mean Anomaly}

From Kepler's equation to first order in $e$:
\begin{equation}
\cos E \approx \cos M - \frac{e}{2} + \frac{e}{2}\cos 2M + O(e^2)
\end{equation}

When averaged: $\langle \cos E \rangle = -e/2 + O(e^2)$ (constant term survives).
