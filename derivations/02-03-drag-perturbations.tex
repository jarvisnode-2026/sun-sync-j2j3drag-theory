\section{Objective}
Derive drag perturbation rates for orbital elements using Gauss equations.

\section{Gauss Equations for Drag}

With $a_R = 0$, $a_N = 0$, $a_T = -\frac{1}{2}\rho v^2 B$ (tangential, opposing motion):

\subsection{Semi-Major Axis}
\begin{align}
\frac{da}{dt} &= \frac{2a^2}{h}\frac{p}{r} a_T\\
&= \frac{2a^2}{\sqrt{\mu a(1-e^2)}} \cdot \frac{a(1-e^2)}{a(1-e\cos E)} \cdot a_T\\
&= \frac{2a^{3/2}\sqrt{1-e^2}}{\sqrt{\mu}(1-e\cos E)} a_T
\end{align}

For circular orbits ($e = 0$, $r = a$):
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\left.\frac{da}{dt}\right|_{\text{drag, circular}} = \frac{2a^{3/2}}{\sqrt{\mu}} a_T = -\frac{a^{3/2}}{\sqrt{\mu}}\rho v^2 B}
\end{equation}

Substituting $v^2 = \mu/a$ for circular:
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\left.\frac{da}{dt}\right|_{\text{drag, circular}} = -a^{1/2}\rho B\sqrt{\mu}}
\label{eq:dadt_drag}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Eccentricity}
\begin{equation}
\frac{de}{dt} = \frac{1}{h}(1 + \cos E) a_T
\end{equation}

For circular orbits ($e \to 0$): this rate is small (proportional to existing eccentricity).

\subsection{Inclination}
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\frac{di}{dt} = 0}
\end{equation}

Symmetric drag (no normal component) does not change inclination.

\section{Combined with Density Model}

Substituting $\rho = \rho_0 \exp(-(a - R_E)/H)$ into Eq.~\eqref{eq:dadt_drag}:

\begin{equation}
\boxed{\frac{da}{dt} = -\rho_0 B\sqrt{\mu a}\,\exp\left(-\frac{a - R_E}{H}\right)}
\label{eq:dadt_full}
\end{equation}

This is the **secular drag rate** for semi-major axis (circular orbit approximation).

\section{Physical Interpretation}

- $da/dt < 0$ always → altitude decays
- Exponential dependence on altitude: drag increases rapidly at lower altitudes
- Proportional to ballistic coefficient $B$ (high-area, low-mass satellites decay faster)
